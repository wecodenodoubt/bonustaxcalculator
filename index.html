Why Is My Bonus Taxed So Much? (The Ultimate Guide)

It is one of the most frustrating moments for an employee: you work hard all year, finally earn a $5,000 bonus, and when the check arrives, it looks more like $3,000. You didn't actually lose that money forever, but the tax withholding rules make it feel that way.

The "Bonus Tax" isn't usually a separate tax rate that penalizes you for working harder. It is simply a different method of withholding used by payroll departments to ensure you don't underpay your taxes for the year. However, these methods are often aggressive.

Key Takeaway

Bonuses are taxed as "Supplemental Income." While the withholding (the money taken out now) is higher, the actual tax liability (what you truly owe) is calculated at the end of the year. If too much was taken, you get a refund.

The Two Main Ways Bonuses Are Taxed

Depending on your country and employer, one of two methods is generally used:

  1. The Percentage Method (Flat Rate): Your employer ignores your regular salary bracket and applies a flat "supplemental" rate to the bonus. This is common in the USA (22% Federal).
  2. The Aggregate Method (Annualization): This is the painful one. Your payroll software adds your bonus to your regular paycheck, assumes you make that huge amount every singe pay period, and taxes you as if you are in a top-tier tax bracket.

In the United States, the IRS classifies bonuses, commissions, and severance pay as "Supplemental Wages." This distinction allows employers to withhold tax differently than your standard W-4 paycheck.

1. The Flat 22% Rule

For most bonuses under $1 million, employers typically withhold a flat 22% for Federal Income Tax. While 22% might sound reasonable, it is only one piece of the pie.

The Full Breakdown of Deductions:

  • Federal Income Tax: 22% (Flat rate)
  • Social Security: 6.2% (Up to the wage base limit)
  • Medicare: 1.45%
  • State Income Tax: 0% to 13.3% (varies by state like CA, NY)
  • 401(k) Contributions: Often automatically deducted

When you combine these, a resident of New York or California often sees 40% to 45% of their bonus disappear before it hits their bank account.

2. The "Aggregate Method" Trap

If your bonus is added to your regular paycheck rather than issued as a separate check, your employer might use the Aggregate Method. The IRS tables will tax the combined amount at a much higher marginal bracket, often resulting in excessive withholding that you won't recover until tax season.

In the UK, bonuses are treated simply as income. There is no separate "bonus tax rate," but the mechanisms of PAYE (Pay As You Earn) can create shocking deductions.

The 40% Trap

If your bonus pushes your income for the month into the next tax band, you will pay the higher rate on that portion. For example, if you are a Basic Rate taxpayer (20%) but your bonus pushes you temporarily into the Higher Rate threshold (Â£50,270 annualized), you pay 40% tax on the bonus, plus 2% National Insurance.

Month 1 Tax Codes

Sometimes, a large bonus triggers payroll software to switch you to a "Month 1" or "Week 1" emergency tax code. This treats your pay as if it's the first pay of the year, ignoring your accumulated tax-free Personal Allowance, resulting in massive over-taxation. This usually auto-corrects in the next payroll run, or you can claim a refund from HMRC.

The Canada Revenue Agency (CRA) requires a complex calculation known as the "Bonus Method" for lump-sum payments. This is essentially a marginal tax rate calculation.

How it works:

  1. The payroll system calculates annual tax on your regular salary.
  2. It then calculates annual tax on (Regular Salary + Bonus).
  3. It subtracts the first result from the second to find the tax specific to the bonus.

Because Canada has high provincial marginal rates (exceeding 50% in provinces like Nova Scotia, Ontario, and Quebec for high earners), the withholding on bonuses is extremely high. You also pay CPP (Canada Pension Plan) and EI (Employment Insurance) on the bonus unless you have already maxed out your contributions for the year.

Will I Get a Tax Refund?

The good news: Yes, in most cases, the "Bonus Tax" is an over-payment.

Tax withholding is just an estimate. Your actual tax bill is calculated on your Form 1040 (USA), T1 (Canada), or Self Assessment (UK) based on your total income for the year.

Example:

  • Your bonus had 40% withheld.
  • At the end of the year, your actual effective tax rate (based on your total salary) is 24%.
  • The government owes you the 16% difference. You will receive this as part of your tax refund.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is my bonus taxed at a higher rate than my salary?
Technically, no. It is withheld at a higher rate, but it is taxed as ordinary income. At the end of the year, a dollar of bonus is treated exactly the same as a dollar of salary.
Can I ask my employer to tax my bonus less?
Generally, no. Employers are legally required to follow strict IRS/HMRC/CRA withholding tables. However, you can sometimes adjust your W-4 (USA) or TD1 (Canada) for the rest of the year to balance it out.
How can I lower tax on my bonus?
The best strategy is tax deferral. In the USA, increase your 401(k) contribution for the bonus period. In Canada, put the bonus directly into an RRSP to waive tax at the source. In the UK, use "Salary Sacrifice" into your pension.
Does the Aggregate Method hurt more?
Yes. If your employer combines your bonus with your regular paycheck and taxes the total sum (Aggregate Method), it looks like you make a massive salary, triggering much higher withholding brackets than the flat percentage method.
Why did I get taxed 45% in New York/California?
High-tax states add their own withholding on top of the 22% Federal rate and 7.65% FICA. NY and CA state taxes can add 10-13% easily, pushing total withholding near 45%.
